  • 1633 America's First UFO?, Bay Colony, MA.

    Governor John Winthrop of the Massachusetts Bay Colony records in his journal that James Everell and two other men observed moving lights in the sky earlier that year. He described James as "sober" and "discreet", no doubt to lend credibility to the sighting. He wrote that while James and his two boat mates rowed their way up the Muddy River, they observed a great light in the night sky that darted between them and the village of Charlestown, a distance of about two miles, for roughly two to three hours. He added that “diverse other credible persons saw the same light, after, about the same place." Many consider this to be "America's First UFO sighting".

  • Oct, '48 Fukuoka, Japan.
    Air Force Project Bluebook Oct 15 Incident
    On October 15, 1948, at approximately 11:05 PM, a two-man crew of a USAF F-61 Black Widow night-fighter was conducting a routine patrol roughly 50 miles northwest of Fukuoka over the Korea Strait.
    Radar Detection:
    The crew detected an unidentified target on their airborne interception radar. The object was initially spotted five miles ahead of the aircraft, slightly below its altitude. Visual Sightings: Reports describe the object as a "rifle bullet" shaped craft, approximately 20 to 30 feet in length. Other descriptions characterized it as a glowing, disc-shaped object that appeared to defy conventional physics. Flight Characteristics: The object demonstrated high-speed capabilities and elusive maneuvers, eventually vanishing without a trace.
    Historical Significance: Early Records: This event is recognized as one of the earliest unexplained radar-visual UFO encounters documented by the U.S. military in post-war Japan.

  • Dec, '52 Gulf of Mexico (NOT America)
    Radar Blips/Sightings of UFOs in Gulf of Mexico, off Louisiana coastline
    At approximately 5:24 AM on December 6, 1952, a B-29 crew was on a routine training mission about 100 miles south of the Louisiana coast when they encountered multiple unidentified objects.
    Radar Detection:
    The bomber’s radar operators, led by Lieutenant Coleman, detected several high-speed blips on their scopes. The objects were clocked at speeds exceeding 5,000 miles per hour, far surpassing any known aircraft of 1952.
    Visual Confirmation:
    Crew members looking out of the bomber’s scanners reported seeing blue-white lights that moved with incredible speed, matching the radar tracks.
    Maneuvers:
    The objects reportedly converged on the B-29, slowed down to match its speed for a brief period, and then accelerated away. At one point, the radar showed a large "mother ship" blip that smaller objects appeared to merge into.

  • Jun, '59 Fr William Gill, Boianai, Papau, New Guinea.
    The 1959 Boianai Sightings by Father William Gill (3:38)
    The Incident:
    Over the nights of June 26 and 27, 1959, Father Gill and approximately 25 parishioners at the St. Aidan's Anglican Mission in Boianai reported seeing multiple large, disk-shaped objects hovering at low altitudes..
    The sightings occurred at the St. Aidan’s Anglican Mission in Boianai, Papua New Guinea. The First Night: At approximately 6:45 PM on June 26, Gill observed a large, sparkling object with a "deck" on top. He noted four human-like figures performing tasks on the craft.
    The Interaction:
    On the second night, June 27, the craft returned. Gill and his parishioners waved at the figures; the "men" on the craft reportedly waved back. When Gill signaled with a flashlight, the craft made a swinging motion in response.
    Craft Description:
    The object was described as disk-shaped with a wide base, four "legs" or landing pads underneath, and four panels on the side that glowed more brightly than the rest of the craft.

  • Oct, '67 Shag Harbour Incident, Nova Scotia, CANADA.
    The Shag Harbour UFO Incident
    Shag Harbour UFO is Canada’s equivalent of the Roswell UFO in the United States. On October 4, 1967, an unknown object crashed into the water near the tiny fishing village of Shag Harbour, Nova Scotia. At least 11 people watched the object as it headed towards the harbor. Multiple witnesses heard a whistling sound and a loud bang as it crashed into the water. Shortly afterwards, Laurie Wickens and four of his friends contacted the Royal Canadian Mounted Police after they spotted a large object floating in the Atlantic Ocean about a 1,000 feet from shore.
    The RCMP, the Royal Canadian Navy and the Royal Canadian Air Force became involved in an unsuccessful recovery effort. The investigation revealed that all commercial, private and military aircraft along the eastern seaboard were accounted for. The Navy combed the seafloor of the Gulf of Maine, but found no trace of the object.
